# Risk And Counterevidence

- Thesis review should name the bear case, key risks, disconfirming evidence,
  uncertainty range, and what evidence would weaken or break the thesis.
- Common risk categories include execution, competition, regulation,
  technology, leverage, refinancing, cyclicality, commodity exposure, currency,
  accounting quality, litigation, governance, and liquidity.
- Counterevidence can be more important than confirmatory evidence when the
  thesis relies on a narrow claim or high-conviction narrative.
- Scenario analysis should include downside magnitude, likelihood, time horizon,
  and whether losses are permanent, temporary, or liquidity-driven.
- Risk statements are weak when they list generic hazards without explaining
  how they affect cash flows, valuation, balance sheet, or portfolio role.
- Confidence should be reduced when evidence is stale, single-sourced,
  management-driven, unaudited, circular, or inconsistent with market behavior.
