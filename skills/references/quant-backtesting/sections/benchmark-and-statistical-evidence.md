# Benchmark And Statistical Evidence

- A backtest claim should be compared against a relevant baseline, such as cash,
  buy-and-hold, equal weight, risk parity, sector exposure, factor exposure, or a
  simpler version of the same strategy.
- Uncertainty belongs with reported performance: confidence intervals, bootstrap
  estimates, subperiod checks, walk-forward results, or sensitivity analysis can
  show whether the effect is stable.
- Multiple testing, parameter sweeps, feature searches, universe selection, and
  repeated refits inflate apparent significance unless the evaluation accounts
  for selection.
- Robustness checks should vary plausible assumptions: cost levels, rebalance
  frequency, signal lag, universe filters, volatility targeting, benchmark, and
  sample start or end dates.
- Outperformance claims are weaker when concentrated in one event, regime,
  asset, or short subperiod unless the strategy is explicitly designed for that
  condition.
- Statistical evidence should support the stated claim without converting
  historical association into a promise of future performance.
